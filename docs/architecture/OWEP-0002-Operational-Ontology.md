# Abstract

Operational Worlds are intentionally designed systems through which operational questions may be explored.

This document defines the ontology of Operational Worlds: the fundamental concepts that exist within the framework and the relationships between them.

The ontology is independent of software implementation, fabrication method, or artistic medium. It provides the conceptual foundation upon which the Operational Worlds Design Engine, research handbook, and artistic practice are built.

---

# 1. Purpose

The purpose of this document is to define the conceptual structure of Operational Worlds.

It answers a single question:


**What fundamentally exists within an Operational World?**

The resulting ontology establishes a shared conceptual framework for research, engineering, documentation, and artistic practice.

---

# 2. Scope

This document defines:


• fundamental concepts.
• conceptual relationships.
• dependency structures.
• emergent concepts.
• principles of composition.

It does not define:


• software architecture.
• implementation details.
• programming interfaces.
• visualization.
• fabrication.
• rendering.

---

# 3. Ontological Principles

The ontology is guided by five principles.

## Principle 1 — Concepts precede implementation.

Operational concepts exist independently of software.

The engine is an implementation of the ontology rather than the source of it.

---

## Principle 2 — Concepts possess operational meaning.

Every concept exists because it contributes operational meaning.

Concepts should never exist solely because they simplify implementation.

---

## Principle 3 — Representation is secondary.

A concept may be represented physically, digitally, computationally, performatively, or symbolically.

Its identity remains independent of representation.

---

## Principle 4 — Operational Worlds are systems.

No concept exists in complete isolation.

Operational meaning emerges through interaction.

---

## Principle 5 — The ontology may evolve.

Future concepts may be introduced provided they extend rather than contradict the existing ontology.

---

# 4. Fundamental Concepts

The following concepts are considered foundational.

## Research

The broader inquiry motivating investigation.

Research contains one or more Research Questions.

---

## Research Question

A specific question concerning operational systems.

Research Questions motivate Experiments.

---

## Experiment

A bounded investigation designed to explore one or more Research Questions.

Experiments define context, conditions, and methods of observation.

---

## Operational World

A deliberately constructed operational system.

Operational Worlds provide the environment within which operational interactions occur.

Operational Worlds are the primary object of investigation.

---

## Operational Family

A conceptual grouping of related operational ideas.

Families organize knowledge.

They do not themselves produce operational behavior.

---

## Primitive

The smallest reusable operational construct.

Primitives introduce new operational possibilities into a World.

Primitives are defined by purpose rather than appearance.

---

## Capability

A potential action or influence enabled by a Primitive.

Capabilities describe what becomes possible.

Capabilities are possibilities rather than events.

---

## Mechanism

The process through which Capabilities become realized.

Mechanisms describe operational dynamics.

---

## Constraint

A limitation upon operational possibility.

Constraints regulate what can occur.

---

## Rule

A formal statement governing operational behavior.

Rules coordinate Capabilities, Mechanisms, and Constraints.

---

## Relationship

An operational connection between concepts.

Relationships give Operational Worlds structure.

---

## Operational State

The complete configuration of an Operational World at a particular moment.

States evolve through operational activity.

Experiments observe transitions between states.

---

## Observation

Information gathered during an Experiment.

Observations provide evidence.

---

## Artifact

A representation compiled from one or more Operational Worlds.

Artifacts communicate or embody operational systems.

Artifacts may include sculptures, installations, simulations, publications, datasets, documentation, software, or other forms of expression.

---

# 5. Conceptual Categories

Concepts belong to one of three categories.

## Structural Concepts

These define what exists.


• Research.
• Experiment.
• Operational World.
• Operational Family.
• Primitive.
• Relationship.
• Artifact.
• Observation.
• Operational State.

---

## Behavioral Concepts

These define what may occur.


• Capability.
• Mechanism.
• Constraint.
• Rule.

---

## Emergent Concepts

These arise through interactions within Operational Worlds.

Examples include:


• Competition.
• Cooperation.
• Trust.
• Authority.
• Identity.
• Scarcity.
• Memory.
• Reciprocity.
• Hierarchy.

Emergent concepts are observations rather than primitives.

---

# 6. Dependency Structure

The ontology is organized through dependency.

Research
↓
Research Question
↓
Experiment
↓
Operational World
↓
Operational Family
↓
Primitive
↓
Capability
↓
Mechanism
↓
Constraint
↓
Rule

Artifacts and Observations arise from Experiments rather than existing independently.

---

# 7. Composition

Operational Worlds are compositional.

Primitive combinations generate Capabilities.

Capability combinations enable Mechanisms.

Mechanisms operating under Constraints produce operational behavior.

Operational behavior produces Observations.

Observations inform Research.

No upper limit is imposed upon composition.

Operational Worlds may therefore evolve indefinitely.

---

# 8. Traceability

Every concept should be traceable to the concepts from which it derives.

Examples include:

An Artifact should be traceable to one or more Operational Worlds.

An Observation should be traceable to the Experiment that generated it.

Capabilities should be traceable to the Primitive that enables them.

Research should remain connected to the Questions it investigates.

Traceability is a conceptual requirement rather than a software implementation requirement.

---

# 9. Architectural Implications

This ontology informs but does not determine implementation.

Software architecture should respect the ontology while remaining free to introduce implementation-specific abstractions where appropriate.

For example, software may introduce concepts such as entities, identifiers, graphs, or databases if they improve implementation without altering the conceptual meaning established by this ontology.

---

# 10. Future Development

Subsequent Engineering Proposals will derive their terminology and architectural decisions from this ontology.

Future concepts should extend the ontology through refinement rather than contradiction.

---

# 11. Conclusion

The Operational Ontology establishes the conceptual foundation of Operational Worlds.

It defines what fundamentally exists within the framework while remaining independent of any particular implementation or artistic medium.

The ontology serves as the bridge between research, engineering, and artistic practice, ensuring that each evolves through a shared conceptual language while retaining the freedom to develop independently.
