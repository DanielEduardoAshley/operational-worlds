# Abstract

Operational Worlds are constructed from a shared conceptual language.

This document defines the canonical concepts used throughout the Operational Worlds Design Engine and the accompanying research handbook.

The purpose of this lexicon is to establish a consistent vocabulary so that research, software, documentation, and artifacts all describe Operational Worlds using the same language.

Definitions contained within this document are considered foundational.

---

# Purpose

The Operational Worlds Design Engine is intended to model operational systems rather than geometric objects.

To achieve this, the engine must share a common conceptual language with the research that motivates it.

This document defines that language.

---

# Design Principles

The lexicon should satisfy four goals.


1. Every concept should have one meaning..


1. Concepts should remain independent of implementation..


1. Research and software should use identical terminology..


1. The lexicon should be extensible without redefining existing concepts..

# Core Concepts

## Research

A structured inquiry into the nature of operational systems.

Research defines the questions that motivate Operational Worlds.

Research may contain multiple Experiments.

---

## Research Question

A specific question that an Operational World is intended to investigate.

Examples include:

• Under what operational conditions does competition emerge?

• Under what conditions does trust become stable?

• How does memory influence cooperation?

Research Questions motivate Experiments.

---

## Experiment

A bounded investigation designed to explore one or more Research Questions.

Experiments define the conditions under which Operational Worlds are created, observed, and evaluated.

Experiments generate Observations.

---

## Operational World

An intentionally designed system of operational interactions.

Operational Worlds define the environment within which operational behavior emerges.

An Operational World consists of operational concepts rather than physical objects.

Physical artifacts are representations of an Operational World.

---

## Operational Family

A collection of closely related operational ideas.

Families organize Primitives according to shared conceptual purpose.

Examples might include:

Boundary

Memory

Exchange

Governance

Competition

Identity

Time

Communication

Families provide conceptual organization rather than behavior.

---

## Primitive

The smallest reusable operational construct.

Primitives introduce operational possibilities into an Operational World.

Primitives are defined by purpose rather than appearance.

Examples include:

Establish

Threshold

Archive

Reveal

Exchange

Exclude

Transform

A Primitive may possess multiple Capabilities.

---

## Capability

A potential action or influence made possible by a Primitive.

Capabilities describe what a Primitive enables.

Examples:

Create Boundaries

Store Information

Transfer Resources

Restrict Access

Reveal Information

Generate Identity

Capabilities define potential rather than execution.

---

## Mechanism

The operational process through which one or more Capabilities become realized.

Mechanisms describe how operational change occurs.

Examples include:

Voting

Auction

Encryption

Decay

Inheritance

Random Selection

Negotiation

Mechanisms implement operational logic.

---

## Constraint

A limitation placed upon operational behavior.

Constraints regulate what is possible.

Examples include:

Capacity

Time

Distance

Visibility

Scarcity

Exclusivity

Permission

Constraints shape the operational landscape.

---

## Rule

A formal statement governing operational behavior.

Rules determine how Mechanisms and Constraints interact.

Rules should be explicit whenever possible.

---

## Relationship

An operational connection between concepts.

Relationships describe how concepts influence one another.

Examples include:

depends_on

contains

supports

competes_with

inherits

enables

restricts

transforms

Relationships give Operational Worlds structure.

---

## Observation

Information gathered during an Experiment.

Observations provide evidence used to evaluate Research Questions.

Observations inform future Research.

---

## Artifact

A compiled representation of one or more Operational Worlds.

Artifacts may include:

Physical installations

Sculptures

Games

Simulations

Visualizations

Publications

Datasets

Documentation

Artifacts are outputs of Operational Worlds rather than the Operational Worlds themselves.

# Foundational Principles

The following principles govern interpretation of the lexicon.

Operational meaning precedes representation.

Capabilities precede mechanisms.

Mechanisms operate within constraints.

Rules govern mechanisms.

Artifacts represent Operational Worlds.

Observations inform Research.

No concept should possess multiple conflicting meanings.

---

# Future Development

Future Engineering Proposals may introduce additional concepts provided they remain compatible with the principles established in this document.

Concepts should be extended through refinement rather than redefinition.

---

# Conclusion

The Core Operational Lexicon establishes the conceptual language shared by Operational Worlds research and the Operational Worlds Design Engine.

It provides the foundation from which future software architecture, documentation, experimentation, and artistic practice may evolve while preserving conceptual consistency across the entire framework.
